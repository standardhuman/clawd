## FAQ Entries

### 1. How often should I clean my boat's hull?

Most boats in the San Francisco Bay need cleaning every 4-6 weeks. The Bay's nutrient-rich water grows barnacles and algae fast — a boat left uncleaned for two months can lose 10-20% of its speed and burn significantly more fuel. Sailboats racing or cruising regularly benefit from monthly cleanings. If you're on a mooring or in a slip with less current, you might stretch to 6 weeks. If you're seeing growth on the waterline when you visit, you're overdue.

### 2. What does hull cleaning cost in the Bay Area?

Professional hull cleaning in the Bay Area typically runs $4-6 per foot of boat length. A 30-foot sailboat costs around $135 for a recurring cleaning, or $180 for a one-time deep clean. Pricing varies based on boat type (powerboats cost ~25% more due to running gear), hull configuration (catamarans and trimarans have more surface area), and growth level. Most services have a minimum fee around $150. Subscription plans with regular scheduled cleanings are the most cost-effective — you pay less per cleaning and your hull stays in better condition between visits.

### 3. What's the difference between a diver cleaning and a haul-out?

A diver cleaning happens in the water — the diver goes under your boat and scrubs the hull, prop, and running gear while the boat stays in its slip. It takes 30-60 minutes and costs $100-250 depending on boat size. A haul-out means pulling your boat out of the water on a travel lift, which costs $500-2,000+ depending on the yard, boat size, and what work you need done. Diver cleanings maintain your bottom paint between haul-outs. Most boats haul out every 1-3 years for paint and inspections, but get diver cleanings monthly to keep growth under control in between.

### 4. Do I need hull cleaning if I have bottom paint?

Yes. Bottom paint slows growth — it doesn't stop it. Even premium antifouling paint accumulates barnacles, algae, and slime over 4-8 weeks in Bay Area waters. Without regular cleaning, that growth builds up, reduces your paint's effectiveness, and shortens its lifespan. In fact, regular cleaning *extends* your bottom paint's life by keeping the active biocide layer exposed. Boats that go 3+ months without cleaning often need aggressive scrubbing that damages paint faster than gentle monthly maintenance would.

### 5. How do I know if my bottom paint needs replacing?

Three signs: First, growth returns faster than it used to — if you're seeing heavy barnacles within 2-3 weeks of a cleaning, your paint is likely depleted. Second, the diver reports bare spots, blistering, or paint coming off during cleaning. Third, it's been more than 2-3 years since your last haul-out and repaint. A good hull cleaning service will track your paint condition over time and let you know when it's time. At SailorSkills, every cleaning includes a condition report so you can see the trend.

### 6. What's included in a professional hull cleaning?

A thorough hull cleaning covers the entire underwater surface: hull bottom, waterline, keel, rudder, propeller(s), shaft, strut, and through-hulls. The diver checks zinc anodes and replaces them if needed (at additional cost). After cleaning, you should get a condition report noting growth level, paint condition, any damage observed, and anode status. Some services include underwater photos or video. The goal isn't just removing growth — it's giving you visibility into your boat's underwater condition between haul-outs.

### 7. Can hull cleaning damage my bottom paint?

It can, if done poorly. Aggressive scrubbing with the wrong tools strips paint faster than growth does. That's why technique matters — experienced divers match their approach to the paint type and growth level. Light slime gets a soft pad. Moderate barnacles get careful scraping. Heavy growth may require multiple passes. The biggest risk is actually *not* cleaning often enough. Letting growth build up for months means harder scrubbing to remove it, which damages paint more than regular gentle maintenance would.

### 8. What are zinc anodes and why do they matter?

Zinc anodes (sometimes called sacrificial anodes) are chunks of zinc bolted to your boat's underwater metal — shaft, prop, rudder, hull. They corrode instead of your boat's bronze, stainless steel, and aluminum components. It's called galvanic corrosion protection: the zinc "sacrifices" itself so your expensive hardware doesn't. When zincs get below about 50% of their original size, they need replacing. Neglected zincs can lead to thousands of dollars in prop, shaft, or through-hull damage. Your hull cleaning diver should check them every visit.

### 9. How much does anode replacement cost?

Anode pricing varies by type and size. Small shaft anodes run $30-50 installed. Larger hull anodes (like the ones on saildrives or outdrives) can be $100-150+. Installation adds $15 per anode for labor. Most boats have 2-5 anodes that need replacing every 6-12 months depending on your marina's water conditions, how much shore power you run, and the metals on your boat. A good hull cleaner checks your anodes at every cleaning and replaces them before they're depleted.

### 10. Is hull cleaning environmentally safe?

Properly done, yes. Professional divers remove growth by hand without chemicals. The main environmental concern is bottom paint itself — copper-based antifouling paints are regulated in some California waterways. Hull cleaning actually helps: a clean hull means the boat moves more efficiently, burns less fuel, and the paint's biocide layer works as designed rather than being overwhelmed by heavy growth. Some marinas in the Bay require boats to be on regular cleaning schedules as part of their environmental compliance.

### 12. How long does a hull cleaning take?

For a typical 25-35 foot boat with moderate growth: 30-45 minutes in the water. Larger boats (40-50+ feet) or boats with heavy growth can take 60-90 minutes. Catamarans take longer because of the additional hull surface area. If zincs need replacing, add 10-15 minutes. The diver will also spend a few minutes documenting conditions and taking photos. You don't need to be at the marina — most subscription customers just get a report after each visit.

### 13. Do I need to be at the marina during cleaning?

No. Most hull cleaning customers aren't present. The diver accesses the boat from the dock, dives under, does the work, and sends you a condition report afterward. You should make sure dock access is available (gate codes, etc.) and that there's no gear blocking the waterline. Some owners like to be there for the first cleaning to point out specifics, but after that it's hands-off. That's the whole point of a subscription service — your boat gets maintained whether you're there or not.
